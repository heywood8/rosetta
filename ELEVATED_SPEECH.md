[Problem]
AI coding agents are great - until you try to use them across a real engineering organization.
Every engineer ends up creating their own prompts, rules, and workflows. Knowledge gets trapped in silos. Senior engineers know the architecture and compliance constraints - but the agents don’t.
So instead of thinking carefully, agents often optimize for fast answers. And once you have dozens or hundreds of engineers using different agents - keeping standards consistent becomes almost impossible.
[Solution]
That’s why we built Rosetta.
Rosetta is an open-source governance and context layer for AI coding agents. It’s not another proprietary agent. It works with the tools engineers already use - Claude Code, Cursor, Copilot, and others.
The idea is simple: create one centralized source of engineering knowledge and compile it into every agent consistently. Rosetta gives agents persistent engineering judgment:
rules for always-on standards,
skills for specialized expertise,
hooks for non-negotiable guardrails,
workflows that force agents to ask instead of assume,
and subagents that review and validate each other’s work.
Everything can be versioned in Git and runs inside the client’s security perimeter.
This is a meta-prompting that sits at the core of Rosetta’s idea: teaching agents how to think, not what to think. Modern models already know Python, Java, and React. What they lack is your company’s engineering discipline.
[Proof]
Rosetta is already running in production across multiple enterprise engagements.
On brownfield enterprise projects, once requirements are aligned, we commonly see 2× productivity gains or more - and even higher on greenfield work. And importantly, security, testing, and documentation are not optional checkboxes. They’re enforced directly inside the workflow.
[Summary]
Enterprise AI engineering cannot rely on vibes and prompt hacks. It needs repeatability, guardrails, and shared standards. Rosetta is Grid Dynamics’ engineering judgment and experience, codified. It’s Apache 2.0 licensed, fully open source, and available today on GitHub.
